Mutual Funds

A mutual fund is an investment vehicle that pools money from many investors to purchase a diversified portfolio of securities like stocks, bonds, and money market instruments, all managed by professional fund managers.

How They Work

When you invest in a mutual fund, you buy shares (or units) in the fund, becoming a part-owner of all its underlying assets. The fund manager makes investment decisions (buying and selling securities) on your behalf, aiming to achieve the fund's stated investment objectives.

  • Net Asset Value (NAV): The price of a mutual fund share is calculated once daily, after the market closes, based on the total value of all securities in the portfolio, minus expenses, divided by the number of outstanding shares.
  • Pooling money: A mutual fund collects funds from numerous investors to create a large pool of capital
  • Professional management: A professional fund manager invests this pooled money in a variety of securities, such as stocks, bonds, and money market instruments.
  • Diversification: By spreading investments across many different assets, mutual funds reduce the risk associated with any single investment. If one security performs poorly, its effect on the overall fund is minimized.
  • Professional expertise Fund managers analyze investments and make decisions to achieve the fund's objectives, providing professional management for investors.

How investors make money

  • Periodic distribution: Investors receive a share of the fund's income, which can be distributed on a regular basis (e.g., quarterly, bi-annually). This is common in funds that focus on low-risk money market instruments.
  • Capital gains: The value of an investor's shares (units) in the fund can increase if the underlying securities perform well. If the price per unit rises, you can sell your units for a profit.

Key advantages

  • Diversification: Reduces the risk by spreading investments across various assets.
  • Professional management: Provides access to expert management and research.
  • Accessibility: Allows investors with small amounts of money to participate in a large, diversified portfolio.

Things to consider

  • Returns are not guaranteed: Mutual fund returns are not guaranteed and can be affected by poor performance.
  • Management fees: The fund manager deducts costs for operations, which can vary depending on how actively managed the fund is.
